Step 1

Build a resume from zero

What actually goes on a one-page resume when your experience is "a part-time job and some classes." Answer: more than you think — if you know how to frame it.

The sections you need

A first-gen resume has five parts. Nothing more, nothing less.

1
Contact info

Name, phone, email, LinkedIn URL, city/state. Professional email only — not your middle school username. Create a new Gmail if needed.

2
Education

School name, degree, graduation year (expected is fine), GPA if it's 3.0+. List relevant coursework if you don't have much experience yet.

3
Experience

Jobs, campus roles, research assistant positions, volunteering — anything with real responsibility. You frame it, we'll show you how.

4
Projects

Class projects, personal projects, anything you built or led. This is where you show capability when experience is thin.

5
Skills

Technical tools, languages, software. Don't list "Microsoft Word" — list things that are genuinely relevant to the job.

How to write a bullet point

Every experience bullet follows the same formula: Action verb + what you did + the result or scale.

❌ Weak

"Helped with customer service at the store."

✓ Strong

"Resolved 30+ daily customer inquiries at a high-volume retail location, maintaining 4.8-star customer satisfaction rating."

Strong action verbs to use: Managed, Led, Built, Developed, Coordinated, Increased, Reduced, Designed, Launched, Analyzed, Trained, Supported, Resolved, Delivered.

Cover letter basics

Three paragraphs. That's it. Keep it under one page.

¶1
Who you are + why this role

Name the company. Say why this job specifically — not "I am passionate about your mission." Something real.

¶2
One story that proves you can do it

Pick one experience that maps to the job. Tell it in 3-4 sentences using action + result.

¶3
The close

"I'd love to bring this to [Company] and would welcome the chance to talk." Short. Confident. Done.

Quick rule

One page. Always one page — until you have 10+ years of experience.

ATS matters

Most companies use software to filter resumes before a human sees them. Keep formatting clean — no tables, no text boxes, standard fonts. Keywords from the job description go in your bullets.

GPA under 3.0?

Leave it off. Many companies don't screen for it unless they ask. If they ask, be honest — but don't volunteer it.

Step 2

Find internships — even year one

The biggest first-gen mistake: finding out about internship deadlines a year after they've passed. Here's the timeline — and the programs that actively want you.

The timeline — earlier than you think

August–September
Big tech opens applications

Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta open summer internship applications in the fall — 10 months before the internship starts. Set your calendar now.

October–November
Most corporate recruiting peaks

Finance, consulting, engineering roles. Applications close fast. If you apply in January, many are already full.

November–January
Government, nonprofit, and fellowship deadlines

Many first-gen-friendly fellowships have winter deadlines — NSF, Smithsonian, government agencies, civic organizations.

January–March
Smaller companies and late openers

Not all companies recruit in fall. Startups, local companies, nonprofits often hire in the new year. Handshake is your best bet here.

Programs that want first-gen and freshman applicants

These programs are specifically designed for people with less traditional backgrounds. Apply to all of them you qualify for.

Google STEP Freshman & Sophomore

Designed for first- and second-year students from underrepresented groups. Fully paid, mentorship included.

Microsoft Explore Freshman & Sophomore

Rotating program exposing early students to PM, SWE, and design. First-gen and first-year friendly.

Meta University Freshman & Sophomore

Built for students from underrepresented backgrounds. Opens early fall.

Amazon PROPEL Any year

Amazon's program for underrepresented students. Check Amazon.jobs for current openings.

Forage Any year

Free virtual internship simulations from Goldman Sachs, Deloitte, JPMorgan, and others. Great for your resume when you don't have experience yet.

Your campus career center Any year

Often has exclusive postings from employers who specifically recruit from your school. Most students never walk in. Go in.

Where to actually look

Handshake

The best platform for college students. Many listings are school-exclusive. Set alerts. Apply same day you find them.

LinkedIn

Search "[Company] internship [Year]". Filter by date posted. Message the recruiter after applying — most don't do this.

Company career sites

Go direct. Bookmark the careers page of your target companies and check it every two weeks starting in August.

The real secret

Apply to 30+ places. Not 5. Not 10. First-gen students tend to apply to fewer because they assume they won't get it. The students who get internships apply a lot — and follow up.

Rejection is the job

A 5% acceptance rate means 95 rejections for every offer. That's not failure — that's how recruiting works. Apply anyway.

Step 3

Network without connections

Networking isn't a thing you're born knowing because your parents did it at dinner. It's a learnable skill. Here's the system — with exact messages you can copy.

LinkedIn in 20 minutes

✓
Professional photo

Clean background, good lighting, face clearly visible. Your phone in a well-lit room is enough. No sunglasses, no group photo.

✓
Headline that says what you want, not just what you are

Instead of "Student at State University" → "Computer Science Student | Seeking Summer 2026 Internship | Interested in Product & Data"

✓
About section: 3-4 sentences

Who you are, what you're studying, what you're looking for, what you bring. No corporate speak. Write how you actually talk.

✓
Connect with 50+ people you actually know

Classmates, professors, anyone you've worked with. A profile with 50+ connections looks credible. Start there.

How to message an alum (exact template)

Find someone at a company you want who went to your school. Send this — 90% will respond.

Message template

Hi [Name],

I'm a [year] studying [major] at [your school] — I came across your profile while researching [Company] and noticed you're also a [school] alum.

I'm really interested in [their role / company / industry] and would love to hear about your path there and what your day-to-day looks like. Would you be open to a 15-minute call in the next few weeks?

No pressure either way — I really appreciate any insight you're able to share.

[Your name]

Send it on a Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday. Message 10 people. Expect 3–5 to respond. That's normal — not failure.

The coffee chat → referral pipeline

The call

Ask about their path, what they like, what's hard, what they'd do differently. Do not ask for a job. Listen more than you talk. 15–20 minutes max.

After the call

Send a thank-you note within 24 hours. Reference something specific from the conversation. Keep it short.

4–6 weeks later

Follow up. Share something relevant — an article, an update about your own progress. Keep the relationship warm without asking for anything.

When you apply

Tell them you applied. Ask if they'd be comfortable passing your resume along internally. Most people will — you've already built trust.

Alumni search trick

On LinkedIn: search your school name → filter to People → add a company filter. You'll find every alum at that company. Message 3–5 of them.

The mindset shift

You're not asking for a favor. You're giving someone the chance to help — most people want to pay it forward. The cringe is in your head.

Step 4

Nail the interview

Common questions, how to answer them, what to wear, how to talk about yourself without cringing, and what to say when they ask about money.

The STAR method

Every behavioral question — "tell me about a time when..." — gets answered with STAR. It's the structure interviewers are trained to look for.

S Situation

Set the scene. Where were you, what was the context?

T Task

What was your specific responsibility or challenge?

A Action

What did you specifically do? Use "I" not "we."

R Result

What happened because of your actions? Numbers if possible.

The questions you will definitely get

"Tell me about yourself."

Two minutes max. Present → past → future. "I'm a [year] [major] student at [school]. I've been focused on [relevant area]. Most recently, [one relevant experience]. I'm excited about this role because [specific reason]."

"Why do you want to work here?"

Research the company beforehand. Name something specific — a product, a value, a team mission. Generic answers get filtered out. "I admire [X]" only works if you can explain why.

"What's your greatest weakness?"

Pick a real one that isn't core to the job, then explain what you're doing about it. "I tend to over-prepare before I feel comfortable delegating. I've been working on trusting my team earlier — and I've seen it pay off when I do."

"Do you have any questions for us?"

Always yes. Prepare three. "What does success look like in the first 90 days?" "What's the biggest challenge on the team right now?" "What do you love most about working here?"

Salary — what to say

If they ask about compensation before an offer, you're not obligated to name a number first. You can say:

Script

"I'm flexible and more focused on finding the right fit first. I'd love to understand the full compensation package once we get further along — what is the range budgeted for this role?"

Research salary ranges first on Glassdoor, LinkedIn, and Levels.fyi. Know your number before any call.

What to wear

When in doubt: business casual. Clean, pressed, fitted. No graphic tees. No athletic wear. If it's remote, still dress professionally from the waist up — it affects how you carry yourself.

Mock interviews

Practice out loud. Record yourself. Most college career centers offer free mock interview sessions — use them. The first time you answer a question should not be in the actual interview.

The pre-call ritual

5 minutes before: stand up, take three slow breaths, and say out loud one thing you're genuinely proud of. You're not performing confidence — you're reminding yourself it's already there.
