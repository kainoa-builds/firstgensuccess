What they're facing

The system your student is navigating

College has a set of unwritten rules. Students from college-educated families absorb these at home. First-gen students have to figure them out mid-game. Here's the game.

What the first year actually looks like

The first year of college is a significant transition — for your student and for your family. Here's what's actually going on behind the scenes:

1
Academic independence is sudden

No one checks if they're going to class. No one reminds them about assignments. The syllabus has every deadline — but only if they read it. The jump from high school structure to college independence is steep.

2
They're managing money — maybe for the first time

Financial aid arrives as a lump sum each semester. Many students have never managed a budget before. This is a new skill, and mistakes here are expensive.

3
Imposter syndrome is real

Many first-gen students feel like they don't belong — like everyone else knows something they don't. This is normal and widespread. The best thing you can do is validate it and remind them they earned their place.

4
They're carrying your family's hopes — that's a lot of weight

First-gen students often feel responsible for their entire family's investment in their future. Be aware of this pressure. Check in on how they're doing emotionally, not just academically.

The four things that move the needle most

FAFSA — every year by Oct 1

This form unlocks free federal money. Students who file late leave thousands of dollars in grants on the table. This is the highest-ROI task every fall.

Internship applications — fall, not spring

Major companies open applications in September for next summer. By the time your student mentions it in January, deadlines have passed. Remind them in August.

Going to office hours

Students who talk to their professors get better grades, better letters of recommendation, and more opportunities. Most never go. Encourage this.

Using campus resources

Career centers, tutoring, counseling, writing labs — all included in tuition. Underused by first-gen students who don't know they exist or feel like they're for someone else. They're for everyone.

The most important thing

You don't need to understand everything about college to support your student. You just need to keep the relationship open, ask how they're really doing, and remind them that you're proud — regardless of outcomes.

When to push vs. give space

Push on deadlines (FAFSA, registration, internship apps). Give space on everything else. They need to build autonomy — your role is the safety net, not the engine.

The words to know

College in plain English — for families

When your student calls talking about "FAFSA" and "prerequisites" and "the bursar office" — here's what they mean.

FAFSA

Free Application for Federal Student Aid. The government form that determines how much financial aid your family qualifies for. File every year by October 1st. Free to submit — never pay anyone to fill it out for you.

Financial aid package

What the school offers your student after reviewing their FAFSA — a mix of grants (free money), loans (paid back later), and work-study (part-time campus job). Compare total cost after aid, not sticker price.

Bursar

The university billing office. Bills come from the Bursar. Ignoring Bursar communication can result in your student being dropped from classes. Open those emails.

Credit hours

How college courses are measured. Most classes are 3 credit hours. A bachelor's degree typically requires 120 credit hours total. Dropping below full-time (12 credits) can affect financial aid.

GPA

Grade Point Average. On a 4.0 scale. A 3.0 is a B average. Many employers screen for 3.0+. It resets each semester, so a rough start can be recovered from.

Syllabus

Every class has one — it's a document outlining all assignments, due dates, exams, and the grading breakdown. Every parent should know their student reads these on day one.

Office hours

Time the professor sets aside each week to speak with students. Not for getting in trouble — for getting help, building relationships, and setting up future opportunities like research and recommendations.

Internship

A short-term work experience (usually 10-12 weeks, paid or unpaid) at a company or organization. The single most important thing your student can do in college for career outcomes. Applications open in fall for the following summer.

Prerequisite

A class that must be completed before another can be taken. Skipping these gets students dropped from advanced courses and can delay graduation.

Academic advisor

A school staff member who helps students plan their class schedule and track progress toward graduation. Students should meet with theirs every semester.

Work-study

A federal program that provides part-time jobs on campus to students with financial need. Flexible hours, usually pays slightly above minimum wage. If offered, accept it.

Pell Grant

Free federal money — does not need to be repaid — for students with significant financial need. If your student qualifies, this renews each year with a new FAFSA. Protect it by staying enrolled full-time.

Year-by-year

What your student needs — when

A roadmap of what happens each year, what the big deadlines are, and the best moment to gently nudge.

Freshman Year
What they're doing

Adjusting to being on their own, building study habits, figuring out which resources exist on campus, selecting a major direction, making friends.

Key deadlines for you to know
  • October 1: FAFSA opens for next school year — file ASAP
  • November–December: Some freshman-specific internship programs (Google STEP, Microsoft Explore) open applications
  • Spring: Course registration for next fall — advisor meeting needed
Best way to help

Check in emotionally. Make sure FAFSA is filed. Celebrate small wins — finishing a hard exam, making a friend, going to office hours for the first time.

Sophomore Year
What they're doing

Declaring or solidifying a major, building their resume, applying for their first internship, getting more serious about career direction.

Key deadlines for you to know
  • August–September: Big tech internship applications open
  • October–November: Corporate internship recruiting peaks — resume must be ready
  • October 1: FAFSA renewal — same as every year
Best way to help

Ask about internship applications. Remind them about FAFSA in October. Don't panic if they don't know exactly what they want to do — that's developmentally normal at this stage.

Junior Year
What they're doing

Completing a meaningful internship, networking seriously, building toward a return offer or a senior-year job search. GPA matters more now as a screening tool.

Key deadlines for you to know
  • August–October: Summer internship applications for junior-year summer
  • October 1: FAFSA renewal
  • Spring: Some full-time job offers begin arriving for next year
Best way to help

Ask about their internship experience. Encourage networking conversations. Understand that this is a high-pressure year — they're trying to prove themselves to future employers while keeping grades up.

Senior Year
What they're doing

Full-time job search, possible return offer from junior internship, graduation planning, loan exit counseling, transition planning.

Key deadlines for you to know
  • September–November: Most full-time recruiting happens here — earlier than most people expect
  • Before graduation: Loan exit counseling is required by federal law if they have federal loans
  • October 1: FAFSA if enrolled for any of senior year
Best way to help

Be a steady presence. This year carries enormous pressure — job rejection is common and can feel personal. Remind them that the process takes time, most people need multiple applications, and you're proud regardless of the timeline.

How to help

Support that actually works

You don't need to understand every acronym or deadline to be the most important support system your student has. Here's how to show up.

What helps

✓
Ask how they're really doing — not just grades

Mental health is a real factor in first-gen student success. A student who is struggling emotionally won't perform well academically. Make the emotional check-in as normal as asking about school.

✓
Set one reminder on your own calendar: October 1

Text your student every October 1st: "Did you file FAFSA yet?" That single reminder could be worth thousands of dollars in grants they'd otherwise leave behind.

✓
Celebrate effort, not just outcomes

Rejection from an internship or a lower grade than expected can hit hard. Acknowledge the effort — the application, the study session, the courage to try — not just the result.

✓
Trust the resources — and encourage them to use them

Campus counseling, tutoring, career centers — these exist for a reason and are included in tuition. If they're struggling, encourage them to use these. That's not weakness. That's smart.

What makes it harder

×
Asking if they're going to fail

Framing check-ins around fear of failure increases anxiety. Ask instead: "What's been going well?" and "What's been hard lately?"

×
Pressure about the major or career path

First- and second-year students are supposed to be figuring it out. Pressure to have it all decided early often leads to decisions made for the wrong reasons. Give them room to explore.

×
Comparing to siblings, cousins, or family expectations

Every student's path is different. Comparison — even well-meaning — adds weight to a student already carrying a lot.

From a first-gen grad

"The thing that helped most wasn't advice. It was knowing my family was proud of me regardless — that took the fear of failure off the table and let me take real swings."

— Kainoa Aqui, first-gen grad, IBM PM
