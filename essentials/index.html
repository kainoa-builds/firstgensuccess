Money & Aid

Financial aid, decoded

Thousands of dollars ride on paperwork nobody walks you through. Here's what FAFSA actually does, what free money exists, and what to do when the bill makes no sense.

FAFSA — what it actually is

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid. Filling it out is how you unlock federal grants, work-study, and subsidized loans. Not filling it out leaves free money on the table — literally.

!
File every year — by October 1st

FAFSA opens October 1st for the following school year. Many state grants are first-come, first-served. Early filers get more money. File the day it opens if you can.

!
Use the IRS Data Retrieval Tool

Don't type in your tax info manually — use the tool inside FAFSA to pull it directly from the IRS. Faster, more accurate, fewer flags.

!
If your situation changed, say so

Lost a job? Family income dropped? File a special circumstances appeal with your financial aid office. They have discretion to adjust your package — but only if you ask.

Types of financial aid — what's what

Grants Free money

Pell Grant, state grants, school grants. You don't pay this back. Max out everything before taking loans.

Scholarships Free money

External awards based on merit, need, identity, major, or community. Thousands exist. Many go unclaimed. Apply consistently starting freshman year.

Work-Study Earned money

Part-time on-campus jobs subsidized by the government. Flexible hours, decent pay, and builds your resume. Accept it if offered.

Subsidized Loans Government pays interest in school

Borrow these before unsubsidized. Interest doesn't accrue while you're in school. Repay these last.

Unsubsidized Loans Interest starts immediately

Still federal, still low-rate — but interest accrues from day one. Borrow only what you need.

PLUS / Private Loans Last resort

Higher rates, less protection. Exhaust all federal options first. If you must use private, shop rates and read every line.

The real FAFSA tip

File early — state aid is first-come, first-served and runs out. Students who file in October get more than students who file in March for the same family income.

Your financial aid office

Most students never go. The ones who go get more money. Ask about emergency funds, additional grants, and hardship exceptions. Be direct. They want to help.

Study Skills

How to actually study in college

High school studying strategies don't translate. College moves faster, the stakes are higher, and no one checks in on you. Here's what actually works.

The syllabus is your operating manual

On day one of every class, read the entire syllabus. Put every exam, paper, and deadline into your phone calendar immediately. Most first-gen students fail not because they can't do the work — because they got surprised by a deadline they didn't know was coming.

Note-taking that actually helps you study

1
Cornell Method

Divide your page: narrow left column for keywords/questions, wide right for notes, bottom for a summary. Review by covering the right side and testing yourself with your questions.

2
Write it by hand when you can

Research consistently shows handwritten notes lead to better retention. Typing encourages transcription. Handwriting forces you to process.

3
Review within 24 hours

The forgetting curve is steep. Reviewing notes the same night or next day locks in 80% more than waiting until before the exam.

Office hours — the most underused resource in college

Professors and TAs have set-aside time specifically to talk to you. Most students never go. The ones who go get:

Better grades

Professors see you as a person. They know you're engaged. This affects grading judgment on borderline work.

Recommendation letters

You can't get a strong letter from someone who's never spoken to you. Office hours is how you build that relationship.

Research opportunities

Undergrad research opens doors. Most opportunities come from professors knowing your face. Go to office hours before you need anything.

What to say when you walk in

"Hi Professor, I'm [name] from your [class]. I wanted to clarify something from lecture / check my understanding of [topic] / talk about the upcoming exam." That's it. You don't need to be struggling to go.

Time management basics

✓
Time block, don't to-do list

Put study sessions on your calendar like appointments. A to-do list has no defense against a distraction. A blocked calendar does.

✓
Work backward from deadlines

Paper due Friday? Block time Tuesday to outline, Wednesday to draft, Thursday to revise. Never start the night before.

✓
Protect your sleep

All-nighters reliably tank performance. A rested brain on 7 hours beats an exhausted brain on 12 hours of cramming. Sleep is a study strategy.

Tutoring is free

Your tuition covers free tutoring at the campus learning center. Almost no one uses it. Go especially for STEM and writing-heavy classes.

Imposter syndrome

The feeling that you don't belong or aren't smart enough? Every first-gen student has it. So do most other students — they just don't say it. You belong here. The data backs this up.

Glossary

College, in plain English

Everyone else in the room already knows what these words mean. Now you do too.

FAFSA

Free Application for Federal Student Aid. The form you fill out every year to unlock government money, loans, and work-study. File by Oct 1.

Credit hours

A measure of how much time a class meets per week. A 3-credit class meets ~3 hours/week. You need ~120 credits to graduate with a 4-year degree.

Prerequisite (Prereq)

A class you must complete before you can take a more advanced one. Skipping prereqs will get you dropped from the course.

Syllabus

The contract between you and your professor. It lists all assignments, due dates, and grading policies. Read the whole thing day one.

Office hours

Scheduled time when professors and TAs are available to talk to students. Not for discipline — for learning, relationships, and letters of rec.

Bursar

The university billing office. Where your tuition bill lives. You pay the Bursar. Ignoring Bursar emails can get you dropped from classes or locked out of registration.

GPA

Grade Point Average. A 4.0 = all A's. Most employers screen for 3.0+. If yours is lower, focus it on your major GPA (calculated from only your major classes).

Academic advisor

The person who helps you plan which classes to take and when. Meet with yours every semester. They can catch problems with your graduation plan early.

Registrar

The office that handles enrollment, official transcripts, and graduation records. Different from the Bursar.

Work-study

A federal program that subsidizes on-campus jobs for students who qualify. Flexible hours, counts as financial aid, and looks good on a resume.

Dean's List

Academic honor for students with a GPA above a threshold (usually 3.5+) in a given semester. List this on your resume.

Fellowship

A funded program — usually prestigious — that provides money, mentorship, and experience. Often first-gen friendly. Apply broadly, early in your college career.

Internship vs. co-op

Internships are short (10–12 weeks, usually summer). Co-ops alternate semesters of school and full-time work over 1–2 years. Both count for experience.

Major vs. minor

Your major is your primary field of study. A minor is a secondary concentration requiring fewer classes. A minor can signal interest in a second field.

Waitlist

If a class is full, you can waitlist. Show up to class on day one even if you're on the waitlist — many spots open when students drop.

ATS

Applicant Tracking System. Software companies use to filter resumes before a human sees them. Use keywords from the job posting in your resume to pass it.
